Entertainment for Your Simcha That Actually Feels Natural in English

By Yishai Rasowsky  ·  April 2026  ·  5 min read

You've made aliyah. Your community speaks English at home, in shul, at the Shabbos table. And when you're planning a simcha — a bar mitzvah, a sheva berachos, a Purim party — you want entertainment that feels like you. Not forced. Not awkward. Just genuinely funny and amazing, in the language your family actually thinks in.

That's a harder thing to find in Israel than it should be.

The Problem with Most Israeli Entertainers for Anglo Crowds

Most Israeli performers work in Hebrew. Some will offer to do a show "also in English" — but there's a difference between translating your act and actually performing naturally in a language. Comedy timing, crowd work, the specific kind of banter that builds energy in a room — these things don't translate word for word. When it's forced, everyone feels it.

English-speaking audiences in Israel are also a culturally specific crowd. You understand both worlds — the Israeli and the American or British or South African one. The jokes that land for you, the references that resonate, the way you like to be spoken to — these are real things that shape whether an entertainer truly connects or just goes through the motions.

What It Looks Like When It Actually Works

I grew up speaking English, and I perform in both Hebrew and English naturally — switching mid-sentence when the crowd calls for it, which is exactly how bilingual communities actually talk. For an Anglo crowd, the show runs almost entirely in English. The humor is real, the crowd work is genuine, and nobody is mentally translating anything.

Beyond language, I understand the culture. I know what a frum simcha looks like. I know how to read a room that has the bar mitzvah boy's rebbe sitting next to his American cousins visiting from New York. I know how to be appropriate without being boring, and how to push the energy without stepping anywhere it shouldn't go.

The Events I Do Most Often for Anglo Communities

Bar and Bat Mitzvahs

The bar mitzvah boy becomes the star. I build moments specifically around him — his friends, his family, the things that make him unique. By the end, the whole room has watched something impossible happen through him, and everyone who loves him has laughed and been amazed together. It's a different kind of tribute than a speech.

Sheva Berachos

Intimate settings, mixed crowds, the couple at the center. Anglo sheva berachos crowds often have family visiting from abroad mixing with Israeli friends — I navigate that naturally. The chasan and kallah get a moment that both sides of the family will talk about for years.

Purim and Chanukah Parties

Community events, school galas, neighborhood parties — the kind of evenings where you want everyone genuinely engaged from 8 to 80. Magic and mentalism hit differently when the whole community is in the room together.

Shul and School Events

Melaveh malkahs, yeshiva dinners, school fundraiser evenings — events where the entertainment needs to be classy, engaging, and completely appropriate. I've performed at enough of these to know exactly what that means.
