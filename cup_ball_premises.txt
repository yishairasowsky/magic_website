CUP AND BALL - PREMISE OPTIONS
================================

OPTION A: THE LOST THING (universal relatable humor)
-----------------------------------------------------
The ball represents the thing you can never find when you need it -- keys, glasses, phone.
You've accepted this is your life now.

"I lose things. Not careless -- I mean cosmically. I've lost the same pair of sunglasses
four times. I once found my keys in the freezer. My therapist says it's a metaphor for
something. I've stopped asking. But I've started paying attention to what actually happens
to an object the moment it disappears -- and this ball has taught me more about that than
anything else I own."

The lemon becomes: the thing you find when you weren't looking for it. Deeply relatable.


OPTION B: THE PHYSICS ARGUMENT (smart/funny, good for hitech crowds)
---------------------------------------------------------------------
Your physics teacher told you matter can't disappear, only transform. You failed the class.
But you've been testing the theory ever since.

"My physics teacher said nothing truly disappears -- it just moves somewhere you're not
looking. He also failed me twice, so I've been trying to prove him wrong. This ball is
where I am in the experiment."

The lemon is the punchline: "He was right. I hate that he was right."


OPTION C: THE TODDLER (pure comedy, works at family/corporate events)
----------------------------------------------------------------------
The ball behaves exactly like a toddler. You put it somewhere, you look away, it's
somewhere else.

"I have [X] kids. Once you've raised a toddler, you stop being surprised when something
is exactly where you left it -- because it never is. I've started to notice that this
ball has the same instincts. Watch."

Escalating loads = escalating toddler chaos.
Lemon = "I don't know how this got here. This is just my life now."


OPTION D: THE WITNESS (absurdist/mysterious, strongest for Fool Us or corporate)
----------------------------------------------------------------------------------
You're not doing a trick. You're showing the audience exactly what happens.
The ball is doing something. You're just the one watching.

"I'm not going to make a ball disappear. You've seen that. What I want to show you is
what actually happens -- the moment between where it was and where it ends up. Because
I've been watching this long enough that I think I've finally seen it."

This reframes the whole routine: the audience is now witnesses, not spectators to a trick.
The lemon becomes genuinely eerie rather than just funny.


NOTES
-----
- For luxury/corporate: Option D fits existing script and brand -- sophisticated, slightly unsettling
- For Israeli hitech crowds: Option B lands hard, they love being in on the joke
- For family/mixed corporate: Option C is the safest laugh if you need the room loose
- For Fool Us specifically: Option D is strongest
