# YouTube Interview Prep — Rabbi Reuven Chaim Klein

**Format:** Conversational interview on Rabbi Klein's YouTube channel
**Audience:** Frum-Anglo, Torah-literate, intellectually curious
**Goal:** Entertaining + educational. Position yourself as a knowledgeable frum magician — the kind of guy their shul or simcha should book.

---

## Suggested Structure (~45-60 min)

### Segment 1: Introduction (5 min)
**Q: Yishai, tell us about yourself — how does a frum Jew end up becoming a professional magician?**
- Brief bio: oleh, based in Jerusalem, perform close-up magic & mentalism at luxury events, hotels, simchas
- What drew you to magic — the combination of psychology, skill, and human connection
- Frame: magic as a *craft*, not something mystical

### Segment 2: Kishuf in the Torah (12-15 min)

**Q: The Torah explicitly forbids kishuf. So... are you violating the Torah right now?**
- Great hook question — lets you address this head-on with humor
- Distinction between kishuf (sorcery) and achizas einayim (optical illusion / sleight of hand)

**Q: What exactly does the Torah mean by kishuf?**
- Shemos 22:17 — "Mechashefa lo sechayeh" (a sorceress shall not live)
- Devarim 18:10-11 — the full list of forbidden practices (me'onen, menachesh, mechashef, etc.)
- Vayikra 19:26 — "Lo se'onenu" — the source for the me'onen prohibition

**Q: The Rambam and Ramban seem to disagree about whether kishuf is even real. Can you explain?**
- **Ramban** (Devarim 18:9): Kishuf is *real* — it manipulates actual spiritual forces. The Torah forbids tapping into these forces because they bypass Hashem's natural order.
- **Rambam** (Hilchos Avodas Kochavim 11:16): Kishuf is *sheker v'hevel* (falsehood and vanity). It's all fakery — the Torah forbids it because it promotes false beliefs and idolatrous thinking.
- This is a huge machlokas with practical and philosophical implications.

**Q: So according to the Rambam, if it's all fake anyway, why is it forbidden?**
- The Rambam (Sefer HaMitzvos, Lo Taaseh 32) says even basic sleight of hand — achizas einayim — can be assur because it makes people believe in supernatural powers.
- The issur isn't about the *act* being real, it's about the *impression* it creates.

**Q: But you perform magic tricks! How is that mutar?**
- Key halachic distinction: when the audience *knows* it's a trick, the concern of promoting false beliefs disappears.
- **Rav Moshe Feinstein** (Igros Moshe, YD 4:13): Permits magic tricks done through natural means (sleight of hand) when the performer makes clear no supernatural forces are involved. He finds the prohibitive view implausible.
- **Chazon Ish** (cited in Teshuvos Ve'hanhagos 1:655, Rav Moshe Sternbuch): No prohibition when audiences know it's merely a trick.
- **Rav Betzalel Stern** (Betzel HaChochmah 4:13) and **Kloisenberger Rebbe** (Divrei Yatziv, YD 57): Lenient when audiences understand it's natural means.
- Stringent view: **Shevet HaLevi**, **Yechaveh Daas**, **Yabia Omer** — all magic performances forbidden, even with disclosure. But the widespread minhag follows the lenient view.
- Practical point: At every frum event, the audience knows you're a magician, not a wizard. The context itself is the disclosure.

**Q: What about the Bach's distinction between different types of illusion?**
- **Bach** (YD 179): Distinguishes between sleight-of-hand (physical action = me'onen) vs. pure visual deception without action.
- **Shach** (YD 179:17), **Chayei Adam** (89:6): Sleight of hand transgresses me'onen.
- But Rav Moshe Feinstein argues natural means can't invoke the Torah prohibition — it's about intent and impression, not the mechanics.

### Segment 3: Magic in Chazal (10 min)

**Q: Are there stories in Chazal about magic or magical phenomena?**

- **Pharaoh's Magicians (Shemos 7-8):** The chartumim replicated Moshe's signs — turned staffs into snakes, water into blood, brought frogs.
  - Rabbi Klein's own article: The Torah uses two words — *lahat* and *lat* — for their methods.
  - Rav Shimshon Raphael Hirsch: *Lahat* = using a shiny object to distract. *Lat* = quick hand movements during distraction. Essentially, the Torah is *describing sleight-of-hand technique* thousands of years ago!
  - They could replicate but not remove — couldn't end the plague of lice. Chazal say this shows the limits of kishuf.

- **Arvei Pesachim (Pesachim 109b-112a):** Discussion of sheidim (demons), zugos (pairs — danger of doing things in even numbers), and protective practices.
  - Shows Chazal took seriously the idea of unseen forces, even if the Rambam would later rationalize all of it.

- **Sanhedrin 65b-68a:** The sugya on kishuf — includes stories of Amoraim who could create animals, manipulate nature. R' Chanina and R' Oshaya would create a calf every Erev Shabbos using Sefer Yetzirah.
  - Raises the question: is this kishuf or kedusha? The gemara distinguishes.

- **Chullin 105b:** Abaye's nursemaid taught him folk remedies and practices related to sheidim — shows how intertwined these beliefs were in daily life.

### Segment 4: How Modern Magic Actually Works (10 min)

**Q: So how does modern magic actually work? What's really going on?**
- It's a *collaboration* — professional magicians don't work alone. There are consultant teams, creators, and method designers behind the scenes.
- Categories of method:
  - **Sleight of hand** — pure manual dexterity (card work, coin work)
  - **Psychological manipulation** — misdirection, suggestion, forcing
  - **Gimmicks & technology** — specially made props, electronics
  - **Mentalism** — appears to be mind-reading but uses techniques like cold reading, hot reading, billets, dual reality
- The audience sees a 3-minute trick; behind it might be years of practice and development.
- Analogy for the frum audience: like a good drasha — what looks effortless on Shabbos morning took hours of preparation.

**Q: Is there an element of psychology or human nature that makes magic work?**
- Absolutely — magic exploits how the brain processes information. Attention is selective; memory is reconstructive.
- People *want* to be amazed. There's a willing suspension of disbelief.
- This connects back to the Torah's concern: the human mind is naturally susceptible to being fooled, which is precisely why the Torah warns against those who would exploit that.

### Segment 5: Famous Magicians & Their Stories (10 min)

**Q: Who are some of the most famous magicians in history, and what can we learn from them?**

- **Harry Houdini (1874-1926):**
  - Born Erik Weisz — Hungarian Jewish family.
  - Famous for escapes, but spent his later years *debunking psychics and mediums* who preyed on grieving people.
  - Attended seances undercover and exposed the frauds. Wrote "A Magician Among the Spirits."
  - Connection to Torah values: using knowledge of deception to *protect* the vulnerable, not exploit them. Very much in the spirit of the Rambam's concern.

- **James Randi (1928-2020):**
  - Offered a $1 million prize to anyone who could demonstrate supernatural abilities under controlled conditions. Never paid out.
  - Famous for exposing **Peter Popoff**, a televangelist "faith healer" who was actually receiving information through a radio earpiece from his wife backstage. Randi recorded the radio frequency and played it on The Tonight Show. Popoff's ministry went bankrupt.
  - Lesson: When someone *claims* supernatural power for profit, a magician is often the best person to expose the fraud — because they recognize the methods.

- **Banachek (Steve Shaw):**
  - As a teenager, he and a friend fooled scientists at Washington University's McDonnell Laboratory who were testing for psychic abilities (Project Alpha, 1979-1983).
  - The scientists had no magician consultants and were easily deceived by basic magic techniques.
  - After revealing the hoax, Banachek helped establish protocols requiring magician oversight in parapsychology research.
  - Lesson: Even brilliant scientists can be fooled because they think like scientists, not like deceivers. You need a magician to catch a magician.

- **Bert Reese (1851-1926):**
  - Master of billet work (secretly reading what someone wrote on a slip of paper).
  - Earned enormous sums from his readings — but notably *never claimed to be a psychic*.
  - Reportedly fooled (or impressed) Einstein himself with his "jackpot coins" routine.
  - Shows you can be astonishingly skilled without crossing ethical lines.

### Segment 6: Revealing Some Secrets (8 min)

**Q: Can you actually show us how a trick works?**

*Note: Choose tricks that work on camera, are visual, and where the reveal is satisfying.*

- **The Jumping Rubber Band:**
  - Simple, visual, perfect for camera. Show the method — it's a beautiful example of how a tiny secret move creates an impossible-looking effect.
  - Good teaching moment: "This is what achizas einayim looks like in practice."

- **String Loop Through the Pinky:**
  - Another clean, visual trick. The secret is elegant in its simplicity.

- **Amazing Jonathan's "Quarter Behind the Ear":**
  - Tell this as a story — it's hilarious and illustrates creative thinking:
  - A.J. wanted to fool a magician colleague who had left the room. So he told everyone at the table to actually stick quarters behind their ears. When the colleague returned, A.J. showed both hands empty, sleeves rolled up — then produced a real quarter from behind each person's ear.
  - The method was hiding in plain sight — on the spectators themselves!
  - Great lesson: sometimes the best magic isn't about the secret move, it's about the *thinking*.

### Segment 7: Personal Stories & Wrap-Up (5-8 min)

**Q: Do you have any memorable stories from your own performances?**

- **The Skeptic Teenager Story:**
  - Mid-show, a teenager tried to expose how a trick works — and she was *right*.
  - Instead of shutting her down, you called her up to the front and made her the star.
  - She became the "judge" for the next trick. The audience loved it — they were entertained by the trick *and* by watching this confident skeptic get genuinely shocked.
  - Lesson: A great magician doesn't fight the audience — he includes them. The magic isn't just in the method; it's in the *experience*.
  - Connects to the Torah theme: the goal isn't to deceive people, it's to create wonder and joy. That's the difference between a mechashef and a performer.

## Key Talking Points to Weave In

- **Magic as a kosher profession:** You're bringing simcha to simchas. The halacha supports it when done properly.
- **Magicians as protectors:** Houdini, Randi, Banachek — magicians have historically been the ones who *expose* fraud, not perpetrate it.
- **The Torah was ahead of its time:** The distinction between lahat and lat in Shemos shows the Torah understood misdirection techniques millennia ago.
- **Your USP for the frum market:** You understand both the magic *and* the halachic framework. You're not just entertainment — you're entertainment they can feel good about.
